## I. The Consent Theater Presents: ABHA — The Health ID Nobody Asked For

**The Announcement (June 2, 2026):**

> "The creation of over 90 crore ABHAs reflects the growing participation of citizens in the Ayushman Bharat Digital Mission," said Dr. Sunil Kumar Barnwal, CEO of the National Health Authority.

**The Translation:**

> "We have now assigned digital health IDs to 900 million people. Some of them know about it. Most don't. A significant number probably got one because a hospital made them, because we just mandated that all medical colleges create ABHA IDs for every patient — inpatients, outpatients, and emergency cases. Because nothing says 'voluntary participation' quite like making it a condition of receiving medical care."

**The Satire:**

Imagine being wheeled into an emergency room with a fractured leg, and the first question the receptionist asks is not "Where does it hurt?" but "Do you have your ABHA ID?" This is not dystopian fiction. This is India in June 2026. The government has mandated that medical colleges generate ABHA IDs for everyone who walks through their doors. Emergency patients. Elderly patients. Patients who have never heard of ABHA. Patients who are unconscious. (Okay, maybe not the unconscious ones. Yet.)

**The consent model** works like this: your Aadhaar is linked to create your ABHA. Your Aadhaar was supposed to be voluntary. ABHA is built on Aadhaar. So ABHA inherits Aadhaar's voluntariness the way a child inherits their parents' debt — whether they want to or not.

Some regions have achieved "100% population saturation." Ladakh, Lakshadweep, Dadra and Nagar Haveli. These are territories with populations smaller than a mid-sized apartment complex. The government calls it saturation. The residents, presumably, call it "that thing they made me do at the hospital."

## II. The Great Aadhaar App Retirement — Or: How to Make 1.3 Billion People Re-register

**The News (June 2, 2026):**

UIDAI is retiring mAadhaar and replacing it with a new Aadhaar app featuring face authentication, QR-based sharing, and "selective data sharing" under a "consent-based framework."

**The Implications:**

Let's pause and appreciate what "retiring an app" means when that app is the primary gateway to your digital identity for 1.3 billion people. It means:

1. **Your old profile doesn't transfer.** You must register fresh. Because nothing says "your data is safe with us" quite like losing all of it during an app update.
2. **Face authentication is now standard.** The government that once said "Aadhaar is just a number, not surveillance" now wants your face. As a feature. For your convenience. You're welcome.
3. **"Selective data sharing"** means you can choose what to share — from a menu of options the government has pre-selected. It's like being offered a "choice" of prison meals: you can pick the dal or the dal, but you're eating the dal.
4. **"Consent-based framework"** is the phrase that launched a thousand dystopian novels. In India, "consent" means: we will ask for your permission, and if you say no, the service won't work. That's consent in the same way that "your money or your life" is a negotiation.

The government's earlier proposal to **pre-install the Aadhaar app on all smartphones** was withdrawn after industry pushback. The government called it a "consultation." The industry called it "you can't force us to bundle your surveillance app." The government settled for the next best thing: making the app so essential that not having it is functionally the same as not existing.

## III. Digi Yatra: 10 Crore Smiles and Counting

**The Milestone (June 2026):**

Digi Yatra has crossed 10 crore (100 million) passenger journeys across 38 airports. Twenty-seven more airports are being added. It is one of the world's largest deployments of facial recognition in public infrastructure.

**The Frame:**

"Privacy-by-design architecture," says the government. Passenger credentials remain encrypted on the user's device. Data is shared only temporarily with the departure airport.

**The Reality Check:**

Privacy-by-design is a wonderful concept. It means the architects thought about privacy while designing the system. What it doesn't mean is that privacy was the priority. The priority was moving 10 crore people through airports without hiring more security staff. Privacy was the design constraint, not the design goal.

Here's the thing about facial recognition at airports: it normalizes the technology. Today it's airports. Tomorrow it's railway stations. Next year it's bus stops. Five years from now, you can't enter any public space without your face being scanned, compared, and logged. The infrastructure is being built one "convenience" at a time, and each one makes the next one easier to justify.

**The Bureaucratic Testimonial:**

> "When we launched Digi Yatra, we said it was for passenger convenience. Now that it's at 38 airports, we say it's for national security. By the time it's at 200 airports, we'll say it's just how things work. The beauty of incremental surveillance is that no single step feels like a step." — *Anonymous Ministry of Civil Aviation official (fictional, obviously. They'd never say this on record.)*

## IV. The Digital Divide Daily: 23 Billion Transactions the Unconnected Will Never Make

**The Celebration:**

UPI processed 23.2 billion transactions worth ₹29.90 lakh crore in May 2026. It accounts for 86% of all retail payment transactions. India is the fifth most digitalized economy in the world.

**The Exclusion:**

- 450 million Indians still lack internet access
- Rural internet penetration: 58.48% (urban: 131.86%)
- 60% of gram panchayats lack high-speed broadband
- Women's internet access is 30-40% lower than men's
- Most government portals require English or Hindi — excluding hundreds of millions of regional language speakers
- Aadhaar biometric authentication systematically fails for the elderly, manual labourers, and those with worn fingerprints — precisely the populations most dependent on government welfare

**The Satire:**

India processes more real-time digital payments than the rest of the world combined. It also has more people who can't access those payments than most countries have total population. This is not a contradiction. This is the Digital Indian Dream: a world-class digital infrastructure built on top of a world-class exclusion problem.

The government's solution to the digital divide is Common Service Centres (CSCs) — 5.34 lakh of them, staffed by local operators who perform digital transactions on behalf of citizens who can't do it themselves. This is the digital equivalent of having someone else eat your food because you don't have teeth. The nutrition still doesn't reach you.

**What the 450 Million Unconnected Think:**

They don't think about UPI. They don't think about Digi Yatra. They don't think about ABHA. They think about the fact that the ration shop now requires Aadhaar authentication, and their fingerprints don't scan because they've spent 40 years working with their hands. They think about the fact that their daughter's school fees can only be paid online, and they don't have a smartphone. They think about the fact that the hospital won't admit them without an ABHA ID, and they've never heard of ABHA.

They are not "digitally illiterate." They are digitally excluded. And no amount of breathless press releases about 90 crore health IDs changes that.

## V. ONDC: The Open Network That Closed More Doors Than It Opened

**The Investment (June 2026):**

Uber and Paytm each invested ₹60 crore in ONDC. The government celebrated this as validation of its open commerce platform.

**The Irony:**

ONDC was supposed to democratize digital commerce — to break the monopoly of platforms like Amazon and Flipkart and give small sellers direct access to buyers. Instead, the biggest private companies are now funding it, which means they now have influence over the "open" network that was designed to compete with them.

**The Reality:**

Small farmers and sellers still can't use ONDC effectively. They lack digital literacy, smartphones, logistics infrastructure, and the time to learn a new platform while their crops sit in the field. The people ONDC was designed to help are the same people the digital divide has already left behind. ONDC didn't solve the problem — it digitized it.

## Actionable Citizen Rights Advice

**Know Your Rights Under the DPDPA 2023:**

- **Right to access your data:** You can request a copy of your personal data from any data fiduciary.
- **Right to correction:** You can demand corrections to inaccurate data.
- **Right to erasure:** In some cases, you can request deletion of your data. (Note: the government exemption for "sovereignty and security" significantly limits this right for Aadhaar-linked data.)
- **Right to grievance redressal:** You can file complaints with the Data Protection Board.

**Practical Steps:**

1. **Don't share your Aadhaar number casually.** Use the VID (Virtual ID) system when possible. Share photocopies only with masked Aadhaar numbers.
2. **Lock your biometrics** using the Aadhaar app's biometric lock feature. This prevents unauthorized authentication attempts.
3. **Check your ABHA account** if you suspect one was created without your knowledge. You have the right to know what health data is linked to your ID.
4. **Opt out of Digi Yatra** if you're uncomfortable with facial recognition. Manual verification is still available at all airports.
5. **Maintain physical documentation** — Aadhaar card, PAN card, ration card — as backups for when digital systems fail.
6. **Report biometric authentication failures** to your district administration. The Supreme Court has mandated alternative verification options, but implementation varies by state.
