## I. The Consent Theatre Presents: DigiYatra — Now Mandatory for International Passengers Who Voluntarily Have No Choice

**The Announcement (June 8, 2026):**

Starting June 1, 2026, DigiYatra — India's Aadhaar-linked facial recognition airport system — became **mandatory** for eligible international transit passengers at Delhi, Mumbai, Bengaluru, and Hyderabad airports. The Digi Yatra Foundation and the Ministry of Civil Aviation issued no fresh public statement about this shift. The system simply appeared at the gates, like a surveillance chrysanthemum blooming in silence.

**The Translation:**

> "We said DigiYatra was voluntary. And it was — for domestic passengers. For international transit passengers at our four biggest airports, it is now mandatory. We did not announce this. We did not ask. We simply installed the cameras and waited for you to notice. This is what we call 'progressive rollout.' You may call it whatever your lawyer suggests."

**The Satire:**

DigiYatra's journey is the purest distillation of India's digital infrastructure philosophy: **the roadmap from voluntary to mandatory runs through a phase we call "nobody noticed."**

Phase 1 (2022): "DigiYatra is completely voluntary. Opt in if you wish. Your data stays on your device. We promise. It's self-sovereign. Don't you love that word? Self-sovereign. It means nobody is in charge. Not even us. Especially not us."

Phase 2 (2024): "DigiYatra now has 10 million users. Isn't that wonderful? So many people *chose* it. Of course, we also made the regular entry lanes slightly longer and the DigiYatra lanes slightly faster. But that's just coincidence. We respect your choice. The choice between 8 seconds and 8 minutes is entirely yours."

Phase 3 (June 2026): "DigiYatra is mandatory for international transit passengers. We did not announce this. We did not hold a press conference. We simply changed the gate configuration. The non-DigiYatra lane for international transit passengers now leads to a room where a man named Suresh will ask you to fill a form in triplicate. This is not coercion. This is *friction*. Totally different."

The Digi Yatra Foundation claims biometric templates are stored on the user's device, not in a central database. This is the digital equivalent of saying "the bank vault is empty — we keep all the money in the customers' wallets." Comforting, until you realize the system still scans your face, matches it against an Aadhaar-linked identity, and lets you through. The fact that the template happens to live on your phone is a technical architecture choice, not a privacy guarantee. Your face is still the key that opens the gate.

Meanwhile, families traveling with children discovered a delightful new airport experience. DigiYatra authenticates one face per enrollment. No "+1 child" feature exists. A parent cannot carry a non-enrolled toddler through the e-gate on their own face scan. So the "voluntary" system creates a scenario where a mother, holding a screaming infant, a diaper bag, and a boarding pass, must decide: scan through the DigiYatra gate alone and hope airport staff lets her child through the manual lane, or abandon the "convenience" entirely and take the family through the old-fashioned way.

The system was designed for solo business travelers. The rest of India was an afterthought. This is not a bug. This is *design philosophy.*

## II. UPI Goes Global — While Rural India Waits for Signal

**The News (Week of June 8, 2026):**

UPI is now live in nine countries. India and Nepal launched a direct UPI-NPI cross-border remittance linkage on June 6. Cambodia became the ninth country to accept UPI payments. Indian travellers can now scan QR codes in Phnom Penh while a farmer in Jharkhand walks three kilometres to find mobile coverage to check if their ₹500 government transfer arrived.

MeitY Secretary S. Krishnan celebrated India's DPI leadership at a forum, arguing that India needs "strategic autonomy" in biometrics and AI — the same biometrics and AI being used to scan faces at airports and authenticate exam candidates.

NITI Aayog launched a DPI 2.0 roadmap identifying eight sectoral transformations. Nobody asked the 450 million Indians without reliable internet what transformations they'd like.

**The Translation:**

> "India's digital payments are conquering the world. Our students in Delhi can pay for lattes in Paris. Our tourists can buy souvenirs in Cambodia. Meanwhile, 17 Indian states still lag behind in digital infrastructure adoption. But why fix the divide at home when you can expand it internationally? That way, the exclusion is *globally scalable*."

**The Satire:**

UPI's international expansion is a genuine achievement. It is also a masterclass in the Indian state's approach to digital infrastructure: **build for the headline, not for the last mile.**

The government informed Parliament that it has signed cooperation agreements with 23 countries for sharing India Stack technology. Aadhaar. UPI. DigiLocker. The full package. Twenty-three countries looked at India's digital infrastructure and said, "Yes, we'd like some of that." None of them apparently asked about the 17 states where adoption lags, or the informal economy where cash still dominates, or the rural households where a smartphone is shared among six people.

India's digital public infrastructure is becoming a major governance model — as the government's own social media proclaimed this week. "Major governance model" is a phrase that sounds impressive in a PowerPoint. On the ground, it translates to: a ration card applicant in a tribal block being told to download an app on a phone she doesn't own, to access a subsidy she desperately needs, using an internet connection that exists only in theory.

The UPI-NPI linkage with Nepal is meaningful for cross-border workers and families. The UPI expansion to Cambodia matters for Indian tourists. These are real developments worth celebrating — and simultaneously worth interrogating. Because every time the government announces a new international UPI partnership, it should also announce how many villages got their first broadband connection that week. It never does.

## III. UPSC Face Authentication — When the Gatekeeper Gets a Gatekeeper

**The News (June 2026):**

UPSC deployed AI-based face authentication across all 2,072 exam venues for the Civil Services Preliminary Examination 2026. The system processed 12,000 checks per minute during the entry rush. Each candidate faced a 6-to-8-second biometric verification at the gate, confirming whether the person standing there matched the photograph uploaded in the online application form.

**The Translation:**

> "To prevent impersonation in the exam that selects India's future bureaucrats, we have installed AI face-scanning at every gate. The irony of using biometric surveillance to select the people who will one day regulate biometric surveillance is not lost on us. We simply choose not to dwell on it."

**The Satire:**

Consider the circle: A young aspirant — let's call her Priya — spent four years preparing for the Civil Services exam. She studied governance. She studied fundamental rights. She studied the right to privacy (Justice K.S. Puttaswamy v. Union of India, 2017 — she could recite the key passages). She understands, theoretically, that India has no dedicated data protection law for biometric information, that the DPDP Act's state exemptions are sweeping, and that face recognition technology operates in a legal gray zone.

Then she arrives at her exam center and discovers that to write a paper about governance, she must first have her face scanned by an AI system whose accuracy rates, error margins, and data retention policies have not been publicly disclosed.

The UPSC — the body that recruits administrators to serve a constitutional democracy — now uses biometric authentication to verify that the person who showed up is the person who registered. This is reasonable on its face (pun intended). The problem is the precedent: every new domain where face authentication is deployed normalizes the technology, expands the biometric database by implication, and makes it harder to eventually ask "should we be doing this at all?"

When you face-scan at the airport, it's travel. When you face-scan at the exam, it's integrity. When you face-scan at the hospital, it's health. When you face-scan everywhere, it's infrastructure. And infrastructure, once built, is almost never dismantled.

The Supreme Court's own draft AI regulations (released June 5, 2026) emphasize human primacy and ban AI in core judicial decisions. Meanwhile, the executive branch deploys AI at exam gates, airport terminals, and border crossings with no equivalent regulatory framework. The left hand drafts guardrails; the right hand installs cameras.

## Know Your Rights (This Week's Actionable Advice)

- **DigiYatra**: The system is *technically* voluntary for domestic passengers. Use the manual entry lane. Carry physical ID. If an airport staff member tells you DigiYatra is mandatory for domestic travel, they are misinformed — politely insist on the manual lane. For international transit, the mandatory status is evolving; carry physical documents and be prepared for both lanes.

- **Face authentication anywhere**: Ask what happens to the biometric data after verification. Request the name of the system and the data retention policy. You are unlikely to get a clear answer, but asking creates a record of the question.

- **ABHA / Digital Health ID**: You are not required to create one for private healthcare (only government schemes mandate it). If a private hospital insists, ask if it is a government requirement or a hospital policy. There is a difference.

- **DPDP Act rights**: You have the right to know what personal data is being collected, the right to request correction, and the right to request erasure — unless the entity is the state, in which case the DPDP Act's exemptions are broad enough to drive a data center through.

- **The deepfake threat**: Never share your face on a video call with an unknown caller claiming to be from a bank, telecom company, or government agency. Your face cannot be changed like a password.
